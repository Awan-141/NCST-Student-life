Explanation of Heat Transfer

Heat transfer always happens from a hotter object or substance to a cooler one. The thermal energy naturally flows from areas of higher temperature to areas of lower temperature, and this occurs through three main methods:

  • Conduction: Heat moves through a solid from the hot end to the cold end when they are in direct contact.
    Example: A metal spoon in hot soup gets heated from the soup.
  • Convection: Heat moves through a fluid (liquid or gas) as the warmer, less dense fluid rises and the cooler, denser fluid sinks, creating a circulation that spreads the heat.
    Example: Hot air rises from a heater, and cooler air moves in to take its place.
  • Radiation: Heat is transferred as electromagnetic waves (like infrared radiation) and does not need a medium to travel through.
    Example: You feel warmth from the Sun even though space is a vacuum.

Fun Facts

Heat Transfer

  • Metal feels colder than wood at the same temperature because it conducts heat away from your hand more quickly.
  • Thermal imaging cameras detect infrared radiation emitted by objects, allowing us to "see" heat.

Thermal Expansion

  • Railway tracks have gaps to allow for expansion on hot days, preventing the tracks from buckling.
  • Bridges have expansion joints to accommodate the expansion and contraction of materials due to temperature changes.

Phase Changes

  • Water takes longer to boil at high altitudes due to lower atmospheric pressure.
  • Dry ice (solid CO2) sublimates directly from a solid to a gas at room temperature and pressure.
